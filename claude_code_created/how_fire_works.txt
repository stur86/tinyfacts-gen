How Fire Works

Fire is not a thing you can pick up or hold. It is something that happens when stuff gets hot enough to break up and join with the air around it. This joining gives off light and makes everything very hot, and also makes new stuff - the dark bits that fall off, and a kind of air that goes up and away.

Almost anything that can burn has a point - how hot it needs to get before it will catch fire on its own. Under that point, you can hold fire near it and nothing will happen. Above it, the burning starts and keeps going as long as there is more stuff to burn and enough of the right kind of air.

This is why pushing air onto a small fire can sometimes help it grow - you are sending more of the right air toward it. But pushing too much air will put it out, because you cool it down and push the hot parts away from what is burning.

Fire needs three things to keep going: something to burn, the right kind of air, and enough of what made it start. Take away any one of them and the fire goes out. This is how we put fires out: cover them so air can't reach them, cool them down with water, or wait for the stuff to burn away completely.

Fire has let humans do things no other animal can do. We used it to keep warm in cold places, to make food safer and easier to eat, and to form hard things from the ground into the things we needed to do work. Without fire, most of what we have built would not be here.
