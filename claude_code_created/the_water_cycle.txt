Where Water Goes

Water never really goes away. The water you drink today might have been high up in the sky, or deep under the ground, or sitting in a great wide water many hundreds of years ago.

Here is what happens. When the sun makes water warm in the wide water or in a wide still water, some of it turns into a kind you can't see - a water air - and goes up into the sky. High up, where the air is cold, this water air turns back into tiny drops. These drops come together and form the grey hanging things we see in the sky. When enough drops come together and get heavy enough, they fall as rain.

This rain comes together in moving lines of water, in wide still waters, and in the great wide waters. Some of it goes down into the ground, where green growing things pull it up through their long parts that push deep into the ground. Some goes back into the air from the green parts of those growing things.

Then it starts again.

Rain that falls on high ground runs down into moving lines of water. These carry it toward the great wide waters. The great wide waters give it back to the sky. The whole thing keeps going round and round, on and on, without stopping.

Most of the water on this world is in the great wide waters, but we can't drink it because it has too much of a strong white stuff in it. Rain water has none of this - it stays behind when water turns into water air. This is why rain water is safe to drink.

The water that falls today has been going round this same way for as long as the world has been here. There is no new water. There is only the same water, moving.
